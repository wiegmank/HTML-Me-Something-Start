The Beast of The Woods

I wasn't alone

Several years ago, on a cold and rainy Halloween, I went out to a secluded wooded area of Busch Wildlife. I figured everyone would be buying candy and putting final touches on their costumes to get ready for trick-or-treating. I could spend a quiet afternoon at the archery range all alone in the peace and quiet.

I couldn't keep ignoring how cold my damp clothes were wind the wind picked up and the sun began to set. I couldn't shake the eerie feeling that even though I was the only person on the range, I wasn't alone.

I nocked the last arrow, drew back on my bow, and fixed my eyes on the bullseye at the far end of the range. I thought I heard the wind rustle dead leaves on the ground, but kept my focus on the target. I strained to keep the bowstring steady, and heard the leaves rustle in the wind closely behind me. I began a slow exhale, and then, I felt it suddenly pounce and grab my leg.

My arrow flew from my hands and out of sight. It knocked me off balance, and I was barely able to stay on my feet. I whipped my head down to see what sort of beast could be doing this to me. And then my eyes locked with the beasts', and I'll never forget how it reacted to my fear...

The beast let out a whimper and snuggled up against my leg.

Recovery

The beast was soaked from the rain, and only skin and bones. She was so underweight, she must not have eaten for days. There was clearly a pink ring of raw skin around her neck, obviously irritated by a collar much too tight that she somehow freed herself from.

I reached down to give her a reassuring pat on her head. She lowered her ears, and her head cowered under my hand. She began to shiver, but I couldn't tell if it was from fear or the cold rain. We were miles from the nearest house. I opened the door to my truck and she hopped right in, and situated herself right against the dash vents as the hot air dryed her coat.

A quick stop at a nearby vet office confirmed that the beast had not been microchipped.
